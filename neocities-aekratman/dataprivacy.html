What is this project?

For their final ethics project, Abigail Kratman and Kylie Flerlage have worked to create a 'translated' version of a privacy policy document. These are legit quotes pulled from various privacy policies of well-known companies. Hover over them to see a "layman's" version of what it means.

Want more notes on why this site's useful? Check out our README.TXT.

The Unreadables

These two Privacy Policies have important information hidden amongst long, complicated documents. We've taken some important pieces of information from them-- hover over the paragraphs to learn what they mean in a more concise and understandable fashion, along with their effects for marginalized citizens.

We also may collect other information about you, your device, or your use of the services in ways that we describe to you at the point of collection or otherwise with your consent. You may choose not to provide us with certain types of information but doing so may affect your ability to use some of the Services. We also collect information from suppliers, business partners, and similar businesses, including: contact and business information such as name, phone number, email address, and postal address, government identifier, and business name, industry, locations, other information related to your employer; financial and transactional information if needed to make or receive payments; and, communications with us including your name, email address, and phone number and the contents of any communications you have with us.

The Post will collect your data, includng your location and identity facets. Later in this document, they clarify they reserve the right to sell this data to third parties, exchanging your personal information for monetary gain.

"...We may take steps to verify your identity before completing your request. Our verification measures vary based on the risk to the individual and may involve confirming access to the relevant email account, providing a government-issued ID or other identification documents, or providing other account details or information that indicates a valid request."

UMG requires legal identification for users, which can block access for disenfranchised citizens. This is an important part of data privacy to consider-- those without legal identification still deserve access to services.

The Good One

This example from Canvas' privacy policy is susinct and clear. There is no wool being pulled over your eyes. This hover explains what this policy did right.

“If you are an end user of an Academic Institution or Company that uses our products, it means that your institution determines how your personal information is used. This means that your institution’s privacy notice governs the use of your personal information. This privacy notice is intended to provide transparency related to our privacy practices only.We have provided the following summary to help you understand what we do with the information we process through our Products and the relationship between our users and Products.”

Canvas by instructure detailed users (parents, students, teachers), products users may use, and real world examples of what/who’s information can be accessed by who. Also describes what personal information is taken when.

What Can I Do?

Start caring about your data!

Download a VPN from places like Mullvad or Nord. Talk to your politicians and your community about what you want to see from the government-- local and nation-wide-- about privacy online! Become more tech literate! Stop signing things without knowing what they mean!

No matter what: We can build a better world, where opensource software and private data are the norm instead of something we must fight for!
